# Inclusive Language Guide for Job Descriptions

This reference provides guidance on identifying and replacing exclusionary language in job descriptions. It covers gendered, ageist, ableist, and structurally exclusionary patterns with concrete alternatives.

**Key research finding:** Studies (notably the HP internal report and subsequent academic research) show that women tend to apply for jobs only when they meet 100% of the listed requirements, while men tend to apply when they meet approximately 60%. Every unnecessary or inflated requirement disproportionately reduces the diversity of the applicant pool.

---

## 1. Gendered Language

Masculine-coded language discourages women and non-binary candidates from applying. Research by Gaucher, Friesen, and Kay (2011) demonstrated that job ads with masculine wording were perceived as less appealing by women, regardless of the actual role.

### Masculine-Coded Words to Avoid

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Rockstar" / "Ninja" / "Guru" | Masculine-coded, informal, excludes non-native speakers | "Specialist", "Expert", "Experienced professional" |
| "Competitive" | Masculine-coded personality trait | "High-performing", "Results-oriented", "Achievement-driven" |
| "Assertive" | Masculine-coded personality trait | "Confident communicator", "Effective advocate" |
| "Dominant" / "Dominate" | Aggressive connotation, masculine-coded | "Leading", "Excelling in", "Driving results in" |
| "Aggressive" (goals/targets) | Violent connotation, masculine-coded | "Ambitious", "Stretch", "Challenging" |
| "He/him" (generic) | Excludes women and non-binary people | "They/them", "You", "The candidate" |
| "Manpower" | Gendered term | "Workforce", "Team capacity", "Staffing" |
| "Chairman" / "Managerial" | Gendered title | "Chairperson", "Leadership" |
| "Craftsman" / "Workmanship" | Gendered term | "Artisan", "Craftsmanship", "Quality of work" |
| "Man-hours" | Gendered measurement | "Person-hours", "Work hours", "Effort hours" |

### Feminine-Coded Words (less common issue, but noted)

Excessive use of feminine-coded words can signal a lower-status or lower-pay role:

| Pattern | Risk | Balance with |
|---------|------|--------------|
| "Nurturing", "Supportive" only | Implies servile role | Add impact language: "...to enable team success" |
| "Collaborative" without "autonomous" | Implies lack of individual scope | Balance with independent contribution language |

### Pronoun Best Practice

- Use "you" in candidate-facing JDs: "You will design..." instead of "The successful candidate will..."
- Use "they/them" in internal JDs: "The hire will manage their team..."
- Never default to "he" or "she"

## 2. Ageist Language

Age discrimination is prohibited under D.Lgs. 216/2003 (Italy), the Equality Act 2010 (UK), and the ADEA (US). Many common JD phrases are indirect age proxies.

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Digital native" | Implies young age (born after ~1980) | "Comfortable with digital tools", "Digitally fluent" |
| "Young and dynamic" / "Giovane e dinamico" | Direct age preference | "Energetic", "Proactive", "Self-motivated" |
| "Recent graduate" / "Neolaureato" (as requirement) | Excludes career changers and older candidates | "Entry-level", "Early-career", or remove if not essential |
| "Seasoned" / "Veterano" | Implies older age, can signal ageism in either direction | "Experienced", "With deep expertise in" |
| "X years of experience" (as hard requirement) | Proxy for age; penalizes career changers and fast learners | "Demonstrated proficiency in [skill]" or "Experience equivalent to [level]" |
| "Fast-paced environment" (as filter) | Often code for "young people without family obligations" | "Dynamic work environment" with specifics about what that means |
| "Cultural fit" | Often masks preference for same-age peer group | "Values alignment" with explicit values listed |
| "Fresh perspective" | Implies young age | "Innovative thinking", "New approaches" |
| "Overqualified" (in screening) | Age proxy in rejection decisions | Evaluate against role requirements only |

## 3. Ableist Language

Many JDs include physical requirements that are not essential to the role. Under Italian law (L. 68/1999) and the EU Employment Equality Directive (2000/78/EC), requirements must be genuine occupational qualifications.

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Must be able to lift 25 kg" (non-physical role) | Not an essential function for desk-based work | Remove, or specify only if genuinely essential with accommodation note |
| "Must have a valid driver's license" (non-driving role) | Excludes people with certain disabilities | "Ability to travel to [locations]" (allows alternatives) |
| "Stand for extended periods" (non-essential) | Excludes wheelchair users and others | Include only if genuinely essential; add "with reasonable accommodation" |
| "Clean-shaven" (non-safety role) | Excludes people with skin conditions, religious requirements | Remove unless safety-critical (e.g., respirator fit) |
| "Excellent vision" / "Perfect hearing" | Medical requirement without justification | Specify the actual task: "Ability to review visual designs on screen" |
| "Physically fit" | Vague, excludes many disabilities | Specify actual physical requirements if essential |
| "Normal working hours" | Assumes one definition of "normal" | Specify actual hours: "Core hours 10:00-16:00 with flexibility" |

### Essential vs. Non-Essential Physical Requirements

Before including any physical requirement, apply this test:

1. **Is this task performed frequently?** (daily/weekly vs. occasionally)
2. **Would inability to perform this task fundamentally change the role?**
3. **Can this task be performed with reasonable accommodation?**
4. **Can this task be reassigned without significant disruption?**

If the answer to #2 is "no" or the answer to #3/#4 is "yes," the requirement is non-essential and should be removed.

## 4. Exclusionary Requirements

### Unnecessary Degree Requirements

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Bachelor's degree required" (for skill-based roles) | Excludes self-taught, bootcamp, and alternative-path candidates | "Bachelor's degree or equivalent practical experience" |
| "Master's degree required" (without justification) | Inflates requirements, reduces diversity | "Advanced degree preferred" or specify the actual knowledge needed |
| "Degree from a top university" | Socioeconomic bias, no predictive validity | Remove entirely |
| "MBA required" (for non-executive roles) | Credential inflation | "Business acumen demonstrated through [specific experience]" |

### Experience Inflation

**The >8 Requirements Rule:** Job descriptions listing more than 8 required qualifications significantly reduce the applicant pool, disproportionately affecting women, minorities, and non-traditional candidates. This is a key trigger for the requirements inflation check in the JD pipeline.

Warning signs of experience inflation:

| Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| >8 required qualifications | Discourages diverse applicants | Reduce to 5-8; move extras to "Preferred" |
| "10+ years of experience in React" (React is ~11 years old) | Mathematically impossible or near-impossible | Use proficiency level, not years |
| "5+ years" for every skill listed | Not all skills need the same depth | Differentiate: some foundational, some advanced |
| Requiring experience in specific company's tech stack | Limits to ex-employees of specific companies | List the category: "Experience with a modern CI/CD platform" |
| "Expert in X, Y, Z, A, B, C, D, E" | No one is expert in 8 things | Pick 2-3 expert-level, rest at intermediate |

### Insider Jargon

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| Internal project names | Meaningless to outsiders | Describe the technology or domain |
| Unexplained acronyms | Excludes people outside the industry | Spell out on first use or use common terms |
| "Familiarity with our tech stack" | Candidates cannot know this | List the actual technologies |
| Company-specific methodology names | Only known to insiders | Describe the methodology type: "Agile", "Lean", etc. |
| "FAANG experience" / "Big Tech background" | Socioeconomic and geographic bias | Describe the actual skill: "Experience with large-scale distributed systems" |

## 5. The Textio 5Cs Framework

Use these five principles as a final quality checklist for inclusive JD language:

### Clarity
- Every sentence should be understandable on first read
- Avoid double negatives, passive constructions, and ambiguous pronouns
- Specify what "good" looks like rather than using vague adjectives
- Test: could a candidate from outside your industry understand each requirement?

### Conciseness
- Keep the JD to 700 words or fewer (research shows applications drop off sharply above this)
- One idea per bullet point
- Cut filler phrases: "We are looking for someone who is able to" becomes "You will"
- Remove redundant qualifications that repeat the same skill in different words

### Competency-Focus
- Every requirement should map to a specific job competency
- Remove "nice to have" items from the required section entirely
- Avoid personality-based requirements ("must be fun", "passionate") — focus on demonstrable skills
- Test: could you assess this requirement in an interview or work sample?

### Culture-Signaling
- Show, don't tell: "We pair-program daily" instead of "We value collaboration"
- Include specific benefits rather than generic promises
- Mention ERGs, D&I initiatives, or flexible work policies with concrete details
- Avoid empty buzzwords: "fast-paced", "work hard play hard", "like a family"

### Compliance
- Equal opportunity statement present and jurisdiction-appropriate
- No prohibited questions or requirements embedded in the JD
- Salary range included (where legally required)
- Reasonable accommodation language included
- Privacy notice reference for data collection (GDPR jurisdictions)

## 6. Language-Specific Considerations

### Italian JDs

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| Masculine-only job title: "Sviluppatore" | Excludes women by default | "Sviluppatore/Sviluppatrice" or "Sviluppatore/trice" |
| "Il candidato ideale" (masculine article) | Gender-defaulting | "La persona ideale", "Chi cerchiamo" |
| "Lui dovra..." | Masculine pronoun | "La risorsa dovra...", "Dovrai..." (direct address) |
| "Ragazzo/a sveglio/a" | Ageist and informal | "Persona proattiva e motivata" |

Italian law (D.Lgs. 198/2006, Art. 27) specifically prohibits gender-based discrimination in job advertisements. Using gender-inclusive language is not just best practice but a legal requirement.

### English JDs

| Avoid | Why | Use Instead |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "He or she" | Excludes non-binary people | "They" or "You" |
| "Salesman" / "Saleswoman" | Gendered title | "Salesperson", "Sales representative" |
| "Mother tongue" / "Native speaker" | National origin proxy | "Fluent (C2 level)", "Professional proficiency" |
| "Able-bodied" | Ableist | Remove or specify actual requirement |

---

## 7. Quick Self-Check Checklist

Before finalizing any JD, verify:

- [ ] No gendered pronouns (he/she) — uses "you" or "they"
- [ ] Job title is gender-neutral
- [ ] No masculine-coded power words (ninja, rockstar, dominant, aggressive)
- [ ] No age proxies (digital native, young, seasoned, years of experience as hard gate)
- [ ] No non-essential physical requirements
- [ ] Required qualifications are 8 or fewer
- [ ] Degree requirements include "or equivalent experience" alternative
- [ ] No insider jargon or unexplained acronyms
- [ ] Equal opportunity statement is present
- [ ] Reasonable accommodation language is present
- [ ] JD is 700 words or fewer
- [ ] Every requirement can be assessed in an interview or work sample
